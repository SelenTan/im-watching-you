My cat is a ginger cat, and his name is yet a mystery to find out.

In Chinese, his name sounds like **Tian Ju**. The important thing to know is that Chinese has many words that sound the same but use different characters, and different characters can completely change the meaning. So one cat name can quietly become a family debate.

To me, **Tian Ju** is **Sweet Daisy** (*甜菊*): *Tian* means sweet, and *Ju* means daisy. The name is soft, bright, and a little dramatic in exactly the right way. It sounds like a cat who deserves snacks, compliments, and possibly a tiny crown.

But my family has never agreed with me.

My dad thinks **Tian Ju** is *田橘*, which means **Field Orange**, as if my cat grew naturally on a farm and was harvested at peak fluffiness.

My mom says it is *甜橘*, or **Sweet Orange**, which makes him sound cute, round, and slightly fruity.

My sister believes it is *田菊*, or **Farm Daisy**: still a daisy, but with stronger countryside energy.

So even though we all say the same name, we are apparently talking about four different cats. Mine is Sweet Daisy. My dad's is Field Orange. My mom's is Sweet Orange. My sister's is Farm Daisy.

This also raises a serious family question: whose cat is he?

I do not think he belongs to my sister, because she brought him home from a garbage bin and then somehow retired from all cat-related responsibilities.

I do not think he belongs only to my mom either, even though she is the one who takes care of him every day, because my dad is the one who keeps his food bowl full.

And I do not think he belongs only to my dad, because I am the official snack and toy buyer, which is clearly a position of emotional authority.

So maybe Sweet Daisy belongs to all of us. Or maybe, more accurately, we all belong to him.

He arrived with one name and accidentally collected four meanings, three arguments, and an entire household of staff. Not bad for a ginger cat. I guess he never really cared about what his name is anyway.
